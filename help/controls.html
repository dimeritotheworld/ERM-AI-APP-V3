Controls Management Guide

Controls are the backbone of risk mitigation. This guide explains how to create, manage, and assess controls to effectively reduce organizational risk.

What are Controls?

üõ°Ô∏è
Definition

Controls are measures, policies, procedures, or systems that reduce the likelihood or impact of a risk. They're your defense against potential threats to your organization.

How Controls Reduce Risk

Controls work by addressing one or both components of risk:

üìâ

Reduce Likelihood

Preventive controls stop risks from occurring in the first place.

Example: Access controls prevent unauthorized system access
üõ°Ô∏è

Reduce Impact

Mitigating controls limit damage when risks do materialize.

Example: Backup systems limit data loss from failures

The Risk Reduction Formula:

Residual Risk = Inherent Risk - (Control Effectiveness √ó Coverage)

More effective controls = Lower residual risk

Control Types

Controls are classified by when they act in relation to the risk event:

PREVENTIVE

Stop Before It Happens

Actions taken to prevent a risk from occurring. Most effective and cost-efficient.

Examples:
  • Password policies
  • Approval workflows
  • Safety barriers
  • Training programs
  • Segregation of duties
When to use: As your first line of defense for any significant risk
DETECTIVE

Identify When It Occurs

Actions that detect and alert when a risk event has occurred or is occurring.

Examples:
  • Security monitoring
  • Reconciliations
  • Audit procedures
  • Exception reports
  • Inventory counts
When to use: When prevention isn't possible or to verify preventive controls
CORRECTIVE

Fix After It Happens

Actions taken to correct issues after they've been detected.

Examples:
  • Incident response plans
  • Backup restoration
  • Error correction procedures
  • Disciplinary actions
  • System recovery
When to use: Always have corrective controls as a fallback
DIRECTIVE

Guide Behavior

Actions that provide guidance on expected behavior and standards.

Examples:
  • Policies and procedures
  • Standards and guidelines
  • Training materials
  • Signage and warnings
  • Job descriptions
When to use: Foundation for all other controls
üí°
Defense in Depth: Combine multiple control types for maximum protection. A single risk should have preventive, detective, AND corrective controls where possible.

Control Categories

Categories describe how controls are implemented:

Category Description Examples
Policy Documented rules and guidelines Data protection policy, expense policy
Manual Human-performed checks and reviews Manager approvals, document reviews
Automated System-enforced controls Access restrictions, data validation
Physical Tangible security measures Locks, barriers, security cameras
Segregation Separation of incompatible duties Different people approve and pay
Monitoring Ongoing observation and review Dashboard alerts, trend analysis

Automated vs. Manual

Automated controls are generally more reliable because they're not subject to human error or fatigue. When possible, automate your most critical controls.

Avoid Orphaned Controls

Controls not linked to any risk provide no measurable value. Regularly review your controls to ensure they're all contributing to risk reduction.

Control Effectiveness Testing

Controls are only valuable if they work. Regular testing ensures controls perform as intended.

Effectiveness Ratings

‚úì

Effective

Control operates as designed and achieves its intended purpose. Risk reduction is at expected levels.

  • No significant gaps identified
  • Consistent performance
  • Documentation up to date
‚ö†

Partially Effective

Control works but has gaps or inconsistencies that reduce its effectiveness.

  • Some exceptions found
  • Improvements needed
  • Monitoring recommended
‚úó

Ineffective

Control does not operate as intended. Significant redesign or replacement required.

  • Major gaps identified
  • Not achieving purpose
  • Immediate action needed
?

Not Tested

Control has not yet been assessed. Testing should be scheduled.

  • New control
  • Overdue for review
  • Priority to assess

Best Practices

‚úÖ

Design Controls for the Risk

Match control type to risk nature. High-impact risks need preventive controls; fast-moving risks need automated detection.

‚úÖ

Document Everything

Controls should have clear documentation: what they do, who performs them, when, and what evidence is retained.

‚úÖ

Assign Clear Owners

Every control needs someone accountable for its operation and maintenance. Unowned controls drift and fail.

‚úÖ

Test Regularly

Don't assume controls work. Establish testing schedules and document results. Untested controls may be giving false assurance.

‚úÖ

Review After Incidents

When a risk event occurs, review whether controls failed and why. This is your best learning opportunity.

‚úÖ

Balance Cost and Benefit

Controls should be proportionate to the risk. Don't spend $100,000 on controls for a $10,000 risk.

üéØ
Control Optimization

Look for controls that can mitigate multiple risks. A single well-designed control covering three risks is often better than three separate controls.
